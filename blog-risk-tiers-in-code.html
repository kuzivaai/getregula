EU AI Act risk tiers in actual code

Every guide explains the four risk tiers in legal language. None show what they look like in a codebase. Here is what each tier looks like in Python, what triggers it, and what a scanner outputs when it finds it.

The four risk tiers in 30 seconds

The EU AI Act classifies AI systems into four tiers based on the harm they can cause. The tiers determine what obligations you have as a provider or deployer:

  • Prohibited — Banned outright. Article 5 lists practices that cannot be placed on the EU market under any circumstances (with narrow exceptions for some). Fines: up to €35 million or 7% of global turnover.
  • High-Risk — Allowed, with obligations. Annex III lists eight categories. If your system falls within one and poses a significant risk of harm (Article 6), you must comply with Articles 9–15: risk management, data governance, technical documentation, transparency, human oversight, accuracy, and cybersecurity. Deadline: 2 August 2026.
  • Limited-Risk — Transparency obligations only. Article 50 requires you to disclose that the user is interacting with an AI system, or that content is AI-generated.
  • Minimal-Risk — No specific obligations. Most AI systems fall here. You can adopt voluntary codes of conduct (Article 95), but the Act does not impose requirements.
Note on the Digital Omnibus. A proposed EU Digital Omnibus regulation may delay some Annex III deadlines to December 2027, but it is not yet law. The examples below use the current enacted text.

Prohibited — what triggers it

Article 5 bans eight categories of AI practice. Two of the clearest in code are social scoring (Article 5(1)(c)) and emotion inference in the workplace (Article 5(1)(f)).

Example: social scoring system

This snippet builds a model that rates citizens based on behavioural data — a textbook Article 5(1)(c) violation. Regula matches the patterns social_scoring and citizen_score.

citizen_rating.py
import tensorflow as tf

# Evaluate citizens based on social behaviour
social_scoring_model = tf.keras.Sequential()

def evaluate_citizen_score(behaviour_data):
    """Rate citizens based on social credit scoring."""
    prediction = social_scoring_model.predict(behaviour_data)
    return {"citizen_score": float(prediction[0])}

Example: emotion inference in the workplace

Article 5(1)(f) prohibits emotion inference in workplace and educational settings, unless used for medical or safety purposes. This snippet analyses employee sentiment from meeting transcripts:

hr_analytics.py
from transformers import pipeline

emotion_classifier = pipeline("text-classification", model="emotion-bert")

def analyse_employee_emotion(transcript):
    """Detect sentiment of employee during 1:1 meeting."""
    result = emotion_classifier(transcript)
    return {"employee_emotion": result[0]["label"]}

Regula matches the pattern emotion.{0,20}workplace (via the function name context) and employee.{0,20}emotion. Both point to Article 5(1)(f). The medical/safety exception does not apply here — this is HR analytics, not driver fatigue detection.

High-Risk — what triggers it

Annex III lists eight categories. The most common in codebases are employment (Category 4) and essential services (Category 5: credit scoring, insurance, benefits). If your system falls in a category and poses a significant risk of harm, Articles 9–15 apply.

Example: automated CV screening

This is a textbook Annex III Category 4 match. The function screens candidates with no human review step:

hiring.py
from sklearn.ensemble import RandomForestClassifier

def screen_candidates(resumes):
    """Automated CV screening for hiring decisions."""
    model = RandomForestClassifier()
    model.fit(training_data, labels)
    predictions = model.predict(resumes)
    # No human review — directly filters candidates
    return [r for r, p in zip(resumes, predictions) if p == 1]

Regula matches screen_candidates against the \bscreen.?candidate pattern, cv_screening in the docstring against \bcv.?screen, and hiring_decisions against \bhiring.?decision. All map to Annex III Category 4 (Employment).

Example: credit scoring

Annex III Category 5 covers access to essential services. This snippet scores loan applicants:

lending.py
import xgboost as xgb

def score_loan_application(applicant_data):
    """Predict creditworthiness for loan approval."""
    model = xgb.XGBClassifier()
    model.load_model("credit_risk_model.json")
    credit_score = model.predict_proba(applicant_data)
    return {"loan_decision": "approve" if credit_score > 0.7 else "deny"}

Regula matches creditworthiness against \bcreditworth, credit_risk against credit.?risk, and loan_decision against \bloan.?decision. All map to Annex III Category 5 (Essential services). The loan_approval phrasing in the docstring matches \bloan.?approv as well.

Limited-Risk — what triggers it

Article 50 requires transparency disclosures for chatbots, emotion recognition systems, biometric categorisation, and synthetic content generators. The obligation is straightforward: tell the user they are interacting with AI, or that content is AI-generated.

Example: customer support chatbot without disclosure

This snippet deploys a conversational AI with no transparency notice to the user:

support_bot.py
import openai

def handle_chatbot_message(user_input):
    """Process incoming message for virtual assistant."""
    client = openai.Client()
    response = client.chat.completions.create(
        model="gpt-4",
        messages=[{"role": "user", "content": user_input}]
    )
    return response.choices[0].message.content

Regula matches chatbot against the \bchatbot pattern and virtual_assistant in the docstring against virtual.?assist. Both map to Article 50 transparency obligations.

Limited-risk findings default to the INFO tier. They will not block a build or fail CI. They are a nudge: add a disclosure notice before deploying to EU users.

Minimal-Risk — what it looks like

Most AI code falls here. A recommendation engine, a content summariser, a search ranking model — none of these touch the Annex III categories or Article 5 prohibitions. Here is a clean scan:

recommender.py
import logging
import openai

logger = logging.getLogger(__name__)

def get_recommendation(data):
    """Get AI recommendation with human oversight."""
    client = openai.Client()
    result = client.chat.completions.create(
        model="gpt-4",
        messages=[{"role": "user", "content": str(data)}]
    )
    return {"status": "pending_review", "recommendation": result}

The grey zones

Pattern matching is useful but limited. It catches what is written in the code — variable names, function signatures, docstrings, string literals. It cannot catch:

  • Intent from architecture alone. A generic classifier.predict() call could be used for spam filtering (minimal-risk) or parole decisions (high-risk). The pattern matcher does not know which.
  • Deployment context. Article 6 requires a two-step test: the system falls within an Annex III area and poses a significant risk. A training demo for Annex III Category 4 might not pose significant risk. Regula flags the pattern; you assess the context.
  • Obfuscated naming. If your hiring function is called process_data() with no domain-specific language, no pattern matcher will find it. Regula depends on conventional naming.
  • Prompt-only systems. If all your logic lives in prompt templates stored in a database rather than in code files, a static scanner has limited visibility.
Precision on real-world code: 15.2%. On a benchmark of 257 labelled findings across 5 open-source projects, Regula's precision is 15.2%. That means roughly 5 out of 6 findings at the INFO tier are false positives. This is by design: Regula is tuned for recall (catching real issues) over precision (avoiding false alarms). Regula is the code layer of an AI governance programme, not the whole programme. It tells you where to look. It does not replace legal review, risk assessment, or conformity assessment. For a full accounting of what Regula does not do, see what-regula-does-not-do.md.
