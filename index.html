What Regula actually does

Four things, done well. Not 37 frameworks.

Classification with reasoning

Not just "HIGH-RISK" — why, and what it means

Points to the exact line of code that triggered classification, cites the legal basis, and tells you when it might be a false positive.

regula check . --explain
Documentation that fills itself in

All 9 Annex IV sections — auto-populated where possible

Reads your README, git history, dependencies, function signatures, and logging coverage. Fills in what it can, templates the rest. Covers all 9 mandatory sections.

regula docs --completion
Provider or deployer?

Determines which obligations apply to you

Detects whether you're training models (provider) or calling APIs (deployer). Different roles, different obligations. Most small teams haven't checked.

regula check . --explain
Evidence pack for auditors

One command, one folder, everything they need

Bundles scan results, gap assessment, Annex IV draft, dependency report, audit trail, and remediation plan into a reviewable package with SHA-256 integrity hashes.

regula evidence-pack --project .
Deep EU AI Act coverage

Maps your code to Articles 5–15 and Annex IV. Not a checklist — actual code analysis.

Article 5

Prohibited practices

Social scoring, subliminal manipulation, real-time biometrics, emotion inference in workplaces

Article 6

Risk classification

8 Annex III categories plus medical devices and safety components: credit, employment, biometrics, education, law enforcement, and more

Articles 9–15

High-risk obligations

Risk management, data governance, logging, transparency, human oversight, accuracy — with effort estimates per article

Annex IV

Technical documentation

Auto-populated scaffolds with your actual functions, dependencies, and logging coverage

Article 15

Security

OWASP LLM Top 10 patterns: prompt injection, unsafe deserialisation, unbounded generation

Article 50

Transparency

Chatbot disclosure, synthetic content labelling, deepfake detection obligations
